Rev. Dr. David Lau
Marketplace Pastor

From TV producer to pastor

Many have asked me how I became involved in people’s work lives. It started with a question that popped up on my way to work: “Am I pulling 60 hours a week just to earn a pay cheque?” I asked pastors and seminary professors. I raided the library. There was no simple answer, only more questions.

Given the ethical, moral, and relational challenges in our workplaces, wouldn’t it be better to leave the secular marketplace for church ministry? What did our day jobs have to do with our Christian faith? Was it holier to leave the office early for a prayer meeting? Where was God in our confrontation with management? Did God even care how we went about our daily tasks, so long as we paid our weekend dues? Why did church people avoid sharing about their work when it dominated more than one-third of their lives?

As I wrestled with these issues, I eventually found biblical and theological direction from several people. I’m indebted to Paul Stevens, whose book The Other Six Days reframed my mind to see work as part of our life calling in Jesus Christ and service to the world. I had the honor of meeting theologian Miroslav Volf at a Yale conference in 2005, and of reading his book Work in the Spirit. In that gathering, I heard from many other scholars and pastors who were eager to recover daily work as part of the church’s ministry in the world.

  • Helping people survive an increasingly challenging workplace
  • Discovering purpose in daily work
  • Understanding work as part of our life calling
  • Fulfilling God's purpose of redeeming the marketplace

Over the next 15 years, as part of the pastoral team at Richmond Hill Christian Community Church, I had the privilege of listening to countless stories of success and failure, suffering and sacrifice, indecision and clarity, and regret and renewal from people of the marketplace. I saw lives transformed as the Holy Spirit of God moved people into uncommon acts of kindness.

Some of these stories have been documented as case studies in a research project between 2009 and 2013, called Kingdom Workers. I finished my Doctor of Ministry thesis in 2015, but I’m still gathering more stories to inspire and evoke conversation on Christians in the marketplace!

This website forms the basis of my ministry to everyone who sees more in their daily work than a way to put food on the table. As we explore meaning, mission and ministry in our marketplaces, I pray the Lord’s blessing, restoration, and peace on you and your every endeavor.
